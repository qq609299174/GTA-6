# World Lore

## Setting Overview

Tang Frontier is set in a fictional dark fantasy version of the Tang dynasty.

The empire is outwardly prosperous. Chang'an is wealthy, trade routes are active, scholarship is flourishing, and the imperial order appears stable. Beneath this prosperity, however, ancient systems from previous dynasties are beginning to fail.

The world is built on the idea that every prosperous age inherits buried problems from the eras before it.

The Qin, Han, Sui, and other fallen powers left behind tombs, battlefields, military machines, rituals, sealed spirits, forbidden experiments, and corrupted infrastructure. These were suppressed, forgotten, hidden, or deliberately buried.

The Tang court has maintained public order by keeping these threats out of sight. When the seals begin to weaken, the crisis is treated as a hidden state emergency rather than a public war.

## Core Conflict

The central conflict is not a normal war.

It is a hidden supernatural infrastructure collapse.

Ancient seals, tomb mechanisms, battlefield curses, earth-vein barriers, and underground water systems begin failing at the same time. The court fears that public knowledge of the crisis would destabilize the empire, so it quietly establishes frontier defense settlements in unstable regions.

Players control one such settlement.

Their mission begins as simple reconstruction:

> Rebuild the frontier town and defend it from night attacks.

Over time, the mission becomes larger:

> Understand why ancient forces are awakening and prevent the hidden disaster from reaching the heart of the empire.

## Tone

The tone should combine:

- Tang-dynasty prosperity
- Frontier survival
- Dark fantasy mystery
- Supernatural warfare
- Town rebuilding
- Ancient secrets
- Cooperative pressure
- Folk legend and imperial cover-up

The world should not feel like pure historical realism. It should feel like a mythic Tang world where history, folklore, tombs, rituals, military formations, and fantasy threats coexist.

## Historical Fantasy Rules

The game does not need to be historically exact, but it should maintain a consistent fantasy logic.

### Use Ancient Equivalents Instead of Modern Terms

Examples:

- Supermarket -> Marketplace, market district, shopfront, stalls, city market
- University -> Academy, school, archive, scholar hall
- Hospital -> Clinic, medicine hall, herb hall
- Police -> Guards, patrol, militia, magistrate officers
- Factory -> Workshop, forge, furnace, production yard
- Bank -> Counting house, treasury, money house

### Blend Reality and Myth

The game can include:

- Imperial officials
- Daoist ritualists
- frontier soldiers
- merchants
- scholars
- craftsmen
- caravan guards
- tomb mechanisms
- terracotta soldiers
- ghosts
- corrupted beasts
- ancient seals
- spirit formations

## Major Factions

## 1. The Frontier Settlement

The player's town.

It begins as a ruined military and trade outpost. Over time, it becomes a major defensive and economic hub.

Core identity:

- Rebuilding from ruins
- Practical survival
- Mixed population
- Vulnerable but adaptable
- Becomes stronger through player decisions

Possible residents:

- displaced villagers
- soldiers
- craftsmen
- scholars
- doctors
- merchants
- Daoist practitioners
- refugees
- caravan workers
- rescued NPCs

## 2. The Imperial Court

The official power of the Tang empire.

The court knows more than it admits. It wants the crisis contained quietly.

Possible roles:

- Provides missions and partial support
- Hides information from civilians
- Sends officials to inspect the settlement
- Pressures the town to protect trade and stability
- May become morally ambiguous later

The court is not necessarily evil, but it prioritizes order and secrecy.

## 3. The Bureau of Celestial Observation / Ritual Office

A state-backed group responsible for astronomy, omens, rituals, calendars, and supernatural monitoring.

Possible functions:

- Tracks earth-vein instability
- Studies ancient seals
- Provides ritual blueprints
- Sends scholars and ritualists
- Requests dangerous investigations
- May hide old mistakes

## 4. Daoist Sects and Mountain Orders

Independent spiritual groups with knowledge of seals, demons, ghosts, and ancient rituals.

They may help, compete, disagree, or demand favors.

Possible roles:

- Teach anti-undead techniques
- Unlock ritual buildings
- Provide purification quests
- Guard secret locations
- Disagree with imperial methods

## 5. Merchant and Caravan Networks

Trade factions moving goods through roads, rivers, and Silk Road routes.

They are essential to the economy system.

Possible roles:

- Bring rare materials
- Create trade contracts
- Generate side quests
- Spread rumors
- Unlock distant regions
- Require protection from supernatural threats

## 6. Qin Tomb Forces

Terracotta soldiers and ancient mechanisms tied to sealed Qin military systems.

They are not simple undead. They are ancient war machines and spiritual constructs executing old commands.

Possible behavior:

- Patrol sealed regions
- Attack anyone judged as a threat
- Reactivate production pits
- Defend tomb systems
- Follow ancient imperial orders that no longer make sense

This gives them a distinct identity from normal ghosts or monsters.

## 7. Battlefield Dead

Spirits of soldiers from old wars.

They may appear as:

- ghost infantry
- cavalry shadows
- banner spirits
- cursed commanders
- trapped conscripts
- battlefield drums
- endless marching formations

Their threat is emotional and historical. They are tied to resentment, unfinished orders, betrayal, and repeated war.

## 8. Corrupted Local Spirits

Not all enemies are human-derived.

Possible creatures:

- river ghosts
- mountain beasts
- well spirits
- corrupted foxes or wolves
- shadow insects
- earth-vein parasites
- shrine guardians turned hostile

These threats help diversify regions outside tombs and battlefields.

## Major Regions

The starting hub.

### Theme

Rebuilding, survival, first contact with supernatural danger.

### Key Locations

- Town Hall ruins
- Broken gate
- Old watchtower
- abandoned market street
- dry well
- collapsed workshop
- nearby forest
- small quarry
- ruined shrine

### Gameplay Purpose

- Teach building
- Teach repairs
- Teach defense
- Teach resource gathering
- Introduce NPCs
- Introduce first night attack

## 2. Lishan Qin Tomb Perimeter

A region connected to Qin tomb systems and terracotta soldiers.

### Theme

Ancient military machinery, tomb production, sealed commands, terracotta awakening.

### Key Locations

- outer tomb road
- terracotta patrol field
- broken clay soldier pit
- underground production chamber
- bronze mechanism hall
- buried command platform

### Enemy Identity

- terracotta infantry
- Qin crossbow soldiers
- bronze chariot fragments
- clay war horses
- formation officers
- tomb engineers

### Gameplay Purpose

- Introduce armored enemy counters
- Unlock academy research importance
- Unlock Daoist shrine relevance
- Introduce high-difficulty cooperative dungeon mechanics

## 3. Ancient Battlefield

An old war site where spirits continue marching.

### Theme

Unfinished war, ghost armies, banners, drums, cursed commanders.

### Key Locations

- corpse field
- broken banner hill
- ghost trench
- abandoned command tent
- cursed weapon mound
- midnight drum platform

### Gameplay Purpose

- Multi-lane pressure
- formation enemies
- commanders that buff waves
- side quests involving trapped spirits
- moral choices around purification vs exploitation

## 4. Chang'an Underground Waterways

A hidden region beneath the prosperous city network.

### Theme

Prosperity above, corruption below.

### Key Locations

- old water channels

- hidden official archive
- contaminated reservoir
- maintenance tunnels

### Gameplay Purpose

- Water level mechanics
- corruption management
- imperial secrets
- urban quests
- marketplace expansion through city contacts

## 6. Zhongnan Mountain

A spiritual and late-game region.

### Theme

Daoist knowledge, seals, mountain spirits, purification, truth of the crisis.

### Key Locations

- mountain path
- Daoist hermitage

### Gameplay Purpose

- Advanced ritual systems
- stronger corruption management

## Story Chapter Structure

## Chapter 1: The Ruined Town

Players restore the town and survive early attacks.

Main questions:

- Why was the town abandoned?
- Why do attacks happen only at night?
- What is buried near the old shrine?

Ending reveal:

The town sits on an unstable earth-vein boundary.

## Chapter 2: The Qin Tomb Signal

Terracotta soldiers begin appearing.

Main questions:

- Why are Qin constructs moving again?
- Who disturbed the tomb mechanisms?
- Are the soldiers evil or following old orders?

Ending reveal:

The tomb is not only a burial site. It is part of an ancient containment system.

## Chapter 3: Under the City

The crisis reaches underground infrastructure connected to Chang'an.

Main questions:

- What does the court know?
- Why is corruption spreading through water systems?
- Who benefits from hiding the disaster?

Ending reveal:

The current crisis may have been delayed by previous cover-ups rather than prevented.

## Chapter 4: Broken Roads

Trade routes become unstable.

Main questions:

- Is the threat spreading beyond one region?
- Can trade networks support the defense?
- Which factions can be trusted?

Ending reveal:

The town is one node in a much larger map of failing seals.

## Chapter 5: Mountain Seal

Players seek spiritual knowledge to understand the true source.

Main questions:

- Who built the seal system?
- Why is it failing now?
- Can the system be repaired, replaced, or destroyed?

Ending reveal:

The final threat is not only an enemy army. It is a structural collapse of the old supernatural order.

## Enemy Lore Principles

Enemies should not all be random monsters.

Each major enemy group should have:

- Historical or regional origin
- Reason for attacking
- Visual identity
- Mechanical identity
- Counterplay
- Connection to a building, research, or quest system

Example:

- Terracotta soldiers require armor-piercing or ritual counters.
- Water ghosts increase corruption and target medicine or water-related systems.
- Battlefield commanders buff nearby units and force players to break formations.
- Infiltrator spirits bypass walls and threaten civilians or ritual points.

## Lore and Gameplay Integration

Story should unlock gameplay systems.

Examples:

- Qin tomb research unlocks armor-piercing weapons.
- Chang'an waterway quests unlock corruption reduction.
- Silk Road quests unlock caravan contracts.
- Zhongnan Mountain unlocks advanced Daoist formations.
- Battlefield purification unlocks guard morale and spirit-resistant defenses.

The lore should not exist only as text. It should change what the player can build, fight, research, trade, and explore.
